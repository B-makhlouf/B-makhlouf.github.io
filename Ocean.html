Developing methods to determine the provenance of marine caught salmon

Tools to assess the spatial impact of bycatch

Current methods fail at large spatial scales

Assigning provenance of fish within known river basins has been well demonstrated using otolith isotope values. However, at large spatial scales redundancy in isotope values make it difficult to discern between multiple potential locations of origin. However, to assess the spatial distribution of fish caught as bycatch in the open ocean, we need to develop new methods to assign provenance at large spatial scales.

Can applying machine learning and data science tools uncover new structure in otolith data?

To data, most provenance assignments use the only the natal origin of the otolith isotope data. However, additional data exists in the entire time series of laser ablation data, which records environmental signals from birth until entering the marine environment. By using machine learning and time series analysis tools, we hope to uncover new structure in this additional data which may help us distingish among migration routes in these systems. In doing so, we will develop a model which can reliably assign the provenance of marine caught fish within coastal western Alaska.
