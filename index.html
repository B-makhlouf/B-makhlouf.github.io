Using ear stones to explore spatial patterns in a changing world

Understanding the spatial ecology of mobile species presents challenges to researchers and resource managers. Many of these species travel of vast distances and through rugged landscapes , making manual monitoring difficult or impossible in many cases. This is particularily the case in aquatic species, in which the added barrier of the underwater environment makes monitoring exceedingly difficult. However, many migratory species provide important ecosystem, economic, and cultural services which are under threat from environmental change and human resource use. As such, there is a pressing need to develop and deploy methods which can elucidate patterns of habitat use, migration, and provenance in an effort to design effective management and conservation strategies in a novel future.

I aim to harness the sequential uptake of chemical and isotopic signatures of ear stones of fishes, and otoliths, to reconstruct spatial patterns at an ecosystem scale. Most of my work centers around understanding these patterns in Pacific salmon species in western Alaska watersheds, where they support a multi million dollar commercial industry as well as subsistance opportunities across the region. I use a combination of laboratory experiments, field work, and statistical analysis techniques to develop improvements to existing models and apply them to pressing fisheries research questions in Alaska and beyond. Doing so contributes to a growing toolset of research tools which can be used to address increasingly complex and urgent questions in the field of spatial ecology.

Improving methods for reconstructing provenance

I'm working to improve current otolith-based methods for reconstructing provenance and migration in three ways. Firstly, I am exploring the use of multiple concurrant data sources to improve the precision of basin scale reconstructions and expand their use to new study systems. Secondly, I am using laboratory experiments to confront assumptions of current models about otolith element incorporation and turnover rates. These include testing the effects of diet subsidies on otolith chemistry, as well as the influence of changing water chemistry and variable growth rates. Finally, I am working to apply emerging statistical and data science methods to existing data sets, which may allow us to find additional structure in our existing data and use them to explore new research questions.

Addressing pressing fisheries research questions

As the power of otolith based methods continues to increase, I am working to apply these methods to answer questions of management concern in fisheries research. In western Alaska, the catastrophic run collapse of Chinook and Chum salmon has motivated a large scale research effort to understand the drivers of these declines and ecosystem processes which may help build resilliancy in these populations. I am working to apply otolith-based methods to understand the spatial ecology within coastal alaska watersheds and to better understand the spatial distribution of stressors such as bycatch on these populations. In doing so, I hope to contribute towards the large scale effort to understand and manage these populations in the face of a changing climate and increasing human pressures.
